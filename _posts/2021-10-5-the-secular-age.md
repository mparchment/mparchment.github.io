Taylor defines contemporary society as secular in the sense that belief, even for the staunchest adherent, is simply one option among others. In these circumstances, belief in God is no longer axiomatic, as there are conceivable alternatives available for individuals to choose from.

The difference between the secular and non-secular is one of the background framework in which one believes or refuses to believe in God. The former has opened a question which was formerly taken as a given in the latter by way of the unacknowledged shape of the background. Secularity erodes the immediate certainty of belief, and everyone becomes aware that several options exists, even if one seems more plausible than another.

![Monet, Waterloo Bridge](/assets/img/Monet, Waterloo Bridge.jpg)

The belief of God in earlier periods was undeniable, as so many features of the world then testified in favor of belief, and made the presence of God self-evident:

1. That the great events in the order of the world, such as storms, droughts, floods, plagues, and fertility, were seen as acts of God, instead of the product of a completely natural universe.
2. God was implicated in the existence of society itself. The polity was grounded in something higher than human action in secular time. Besides this, various associations that made up society were interwoven with ritual and worship.
3. People lived in an "enchanted" world; one where spirits, demons, and moral forces had a tangible impact on individuals and entire communities.

These aspects do not only apply to those who believed in the God of Abraham, but also extends to the worldview of various pagan societies. In the enchanted world, agents of good and evil existed that could threaten or help man.[^1] The former included God and his saints, who were prayed to and whose shrines were visited in hopes of a cure. Within this cult of saints, the forces of good also resided in things such as relics or pieces of the body, which had to be treated with care and reverence. Meaning is inherent in these objects, which all have the ability to affect and take over individuals.

Living in this world also meant living socially. Spiritual forces were thought to impact entire societies, and therefore were defended against by entire societies. The "good magic" was that of the Church, which was relied upon for warding off wicked spirits or for the fertility of the land. Rejecting this power and embracing heresy becomes a threat to everybody in these circumstances.

# Structure & Antistructure

During the Middle Ages, Catholicism incorporated an equilibrium based on a complementary hierarchy. The clergy prayed for all, the lords defended all, and the peasants labored for all. The regular order of things was inverted during annual festivities such as Carnival, where people took on the opposite of their typical role. They permitted themselves to licentious or even close-to-violent acts, and so on. These festivals did not put forward an alternative to the established order, but were celebrated in support of the ruling values; as one French cleric said:

> We do these things in jest and not in earnest, as the ancient custom is, so that once a year the foolishness innate in us can come out and evaporate. Don't wine skins and barrels burst open very often if the air-hole is not opened from time to time?

The community was ultimately egalitarian; it included everyone, yet order is also needed. So that order is periodically renewed and returned to its original meaning. All "structure" needs an "anti-structure". Even in societies where an order is harshly enforced, there were moments where it is suspended and transgressed. This was a widespread aspect of many premodern societies.

# Path to Reform

It was usually accepted across human societies based around a "higher" religion for there to be different "speeds" of religious devotion, and can even be seen as a type of exchange between the laity and those who occupy a more renunciated position. However, deep dissatisfaction emerged with the hierarchy defined in Latin Christendom in a way that was unique. It meant that the masses were never going to live up to the demands of the faith, and had to be carried by the perfect—the clergy.

Both the masses and elites therefore sought to bring the two religious speeds closer together, culminating in various different movements.[^2] The Church put efforts into training priests and creating manuals so that the clergy could better form the conscience of the laity.

Mendicant preachers had even more of an impact than the Church did, and were a crucial part in the transformation of medieval Europe. They were responsible for the growing concern and fear of death through endless sermons against the vanities of life, which all disappear at the end. What actually matters is the judgement of God on one's life, which laid beyond death. This emphasis on individual judgement, rather than communal, was a major break in Christian understanding.

This individuality brought about a kind of social bonding, as the living could pray for and bring relief to the dead. The terrifying individual destiny can be met with mutual help. The idea of indulgences finally emerges as the result of the laity's popular piety, who sought to help their loved ones reach salvation.

## Rejection & Disenchantment

"Reform", with a capital 'R', should be distinguished from more moderate attempts to spread practice by preaching, encouragement, and example. What distinguishes Reform from other movements is that the latter does not seek to delegitimate less dedicated forms, but only to bring more people to higher "speeds", or level of devotion. Nonetheless, the Reformation can be thought of as the final culmination of this reform spirit, which produced a uniform community of believers that left no room for different speeds. Every Christian was to be dedicated entirely to the faith in every facet of life.

The spiritual power of the Church was challenged by deep theological objections nearing the end of the Middle Ages, stemming from the nominalism of William of Ockham.[^3] Treating anything as a charged object regardless of the purpose, even the sacraments, was in principle wrong. God's power could not be contained or controlled through its confinement in things, to be aimed by man in one direction or another. The notion that any old priest, however debauched, could control or influence God's actions was unacceptable. 

For the earlier heresies during the medieval period, the sacraments became purely symbolic, authority slid away from hierarchy to be placed back in scripture, and the visible Church was distinguished from the true community of the saved. But it was not until Luther's doctrine of salvation by faith that made the denial of the Church's spiritual power possible.[^4] Luther's message was that every individual was a sinner and deserved punishment. Salvation required facing and accepting this fully. Only in facing one's sinfulness, can one throw themselves at the mercy of God. "Whoever fears Hell runs towards it".

# The Rise of Disciplinary Society

During the Renaissance, the city was seen was as human life at its best and highest. One had to be governed in an orderly fashion, under a code of law, according to which rulers and governors exercised their functions. It required domestic peace, with no tolerance for rowdiness or unauthorized violence among the people.[^5] Elites at the time realized that the masses were still far away from being considered civil. Civility required working on oneself, not just leaving things as they are.

Europe was unique in this regard: the common people were pushed, preached at, and drilled to abandon their disordered folkways and conform to one or another feature of civil behavior. This reform of society, which originally began as an effort to control crime and disease in the increasingly overpopulated urban centers, or for rulers to more effectively extract manpower and wealth from the population, became an objective in and of itself.

## Civility & Reform

The drive for civility was intensified by demands, from both Catholic and Protestant, for religious reform. Hearing sermons and learning creeds were both thought of as integral to civil behavior. Unruly and sinful festivals like Carnival, and the mere presence of anti-structure, had to be censured.

Protestant societies saw the "saint" as the pillar of a new social order, against the disorder and idleness of monks, beggars, and vagabonds. He must give himself a career as a lifetime vocation, which ought to please God. He had to be industrious, disciplined, do useful work, and above all, can be relied upon. They were not tempted to mischief because it is idleness that causes all sorts of evil. Those who did not accept this had to be forced to learn the word of God and live orderly by the authorities until they eventually came about it willingly.

Understandings of poverty in this context were radically shifted. In earlier periods, they were looked upon with pity and were to receive charity, but there was no place for them in the well-ordered commonwealth. Judgement on beggars was scornful: those that were capable of work were expelled or forced to toil, and the incapable were given relief but confined in institutions which resembled prisons. In some Catholic countries, such as Spain, this was too much of a break for the clergy, who put an end to these reforms. But in other regions, like France and Milan, it was not enough and the new approach remained in place.[^6]

The growing sense of man's ability to implement order in his life leads to the notion that there does not need to be any compromise or complementarity. Order does not need to acknowledge any form of chaos, and therefore anti-structures such as Carnival cannot be tolerated. Man loses the sense that there is a limit to malleability in people.

# The Great Disembedding

Early religion thoroughly embedded people. Religion was inherently social because religious action, such as invoking, praying to, and sacrificing to various gods or spirits; coming close to these powers, getting healing, protection from them, or divining under their guidance was directed to the social group as a whole. Collective action was necessary for all this to be effective. It was impossible for someone in this context to think of himself as disconnected from his social environment, as it was part of his identity as a person.

What differentiates archaic religion from higher religions is that people asked for rather natural benefits from divinities, such as prosperity, health, long life, and fertility. The idea that this understanding of flourishing has to be questioned did not exist yet.[^7] Post-Axial religions, such as Christianity or Buddhism, break with this earlier embeddedness. They have novel understandings of the social order, cosmos, and human good. The world in these religions is now considered disordered and imperfect. Salvation, breaking free of the cycle, or imitating the Good becomes the objective, instead of ordinary human flourishing. Something has to be done about evil, which is anything deemed imperfect, and the order of things as they are can no longer just be accepted.

Individualism, as it emerged later, was a result of the attempts in post-Axial spirituality to transform society to reorient itself around these objectives. The modern understanding of the individual is often taken for granted. It required a shift in understanding for this to come about, through the post-Axial religions, which encouraged man to leave family and tribe to follow what the faith demands instead. One is reimagined as a free individual first, as opposed to a member of the tribe or community.

# Modern Social Imaginaries
Social imaginaries comprise the ways people imagine their social existence, how people interact with each other, the deeper meanings underlying these, and how we all fit together. "Social imaginary" is different from "theory".[^8] The latter has only been adopted by a select few or the elites, while the former has popular legitimacy among common people. Though what started as a theory can be adopted by elites and then spread to the masses, after a series of transformations.

The social imaginary also consists of a "background", or the wider grasp of the societal situation including how we stand to each other and how we got to where we are. This understanding is mutually reinforcing with actual practice. There are common actions that everyone knows how to do, and in which cases it is appropriate. Everyone knows what it means and entails to have a demonstration, for example.

There also exists a sense of "moral order", or a sense of what makes these norms realizable. People do not demand for what is utopic. As mentioned before, what was originally idealization grows and becomes the social imaginary, in which case the moral order undergoes transformation.

## The Modern Moral Order

The "Modern Moral Order" was most clearly stated in the various theories of Natural Law which emerged in the seventeenth century. John Locke and Grotius its the most important theorists. Grotius derives the normative order underlying society from the nature of its members.

Humans are rational and sociable agents, who are meant to collaborate in peace to their mutual benefit. The underlying moral order stresses that we have rights and obligations with regard to each other. Political authority is only legitimate because it was consented to by individuals.[^9]

The ideas of moral order derived from the Natural Law theories of Grotius and Locke are different from those embedded in the past. There were two important types of premodern moral order:

1. An order based on the law of a people, which has governed this people since time immemorial and defines the people itself.

2. An order organized around a notion of hierarchy in society, which corresponds to a hierarchy in the cosmos.

Premodern social imaginaries, especially the second type, were structured by hierarchal complementarity. Society was made up of various stations, with a descending scale of dignity, that needed each other. This was seen as the proper order of things, as part of nature. Things were how they should be, and any attempt to deviate from it was to turn reality against itself.

The modern idealization of order breaks from this.[^10] The world as it is no longer defines the good. Men still fill each other's needs and roles complement each other, but these roles have no inherent worth. There is no more hierarchy in society, for all callings are equal in the sight of God.

Modern understandings of order begins from the basis of the individual and his debt to others. Ordered, peaceful, and productive activity is the model for behavior, and men meet in a zone of mutual service. Political society provides security and prosperity, which builds off these pre-political needs.

## Economy as Object

The Lockean idea of order brought about the idea of "the economy". Emphasis was placed on how life produces mutual benefit, and that the purposes of humans mesh together. We are engaged in a mutual exchange of services, and this was to God's benevolent design.

Reformed Christianity placed emphasis on the sanctification and religious importance of everyday life. Everyone had to be completely Christian, no matter their occupation.[^11] More importance is given to the economic in this framework, alongside family life and relationships. The economic is given central importance, and it is thought that the more a society turns to commerce, the more civilized it becomes. Money-making is seen as a "calm passion" that inhibits the violent ones.

## The Public Sphere

Economy was the first dimension of civil society to become independent from the polity, and the "public sphere" was next. The public sphere is a common space where members of society meet through various media. It is a necessary aspect in modern society, and it even has to be faked by despotic regimes.

The public sphere is more than a gathering; it is an abstract, non-local common space, which only exists if it is imagined as such. Various publications and exchanges come to be thought of as one big debate, from which the "public opinion" of society emerges. Printing as a technology was required to get this going, but it had to be taken up in the right cultural context for this social imagination to arise.

The purpose of the public sphere was to have society, enlightened by critical debate, come to a common opinion about important matters.[^12] It is in reason, or even obligated, for government to listen to and follow it. Society can now come to a common mind outside of the political. With the modern public sphere comes the idea that political power must be supervised and checked by something outside. In contrast with premodern society, this check was not the will of God or Law of Nature, though it could be thought to articulate these, but the discourse emanating from public reason.

What makes the modern public sphere distinct from ancient democracy, is it owes nothing to the political. Society in this framework exists outside of the state. It extended to all of what was considered "civilization", such as the whole of Europe. Moreover, it is different from the idea of medieval Christendom because it is entirely secular and not grounded on God or the divine. The public sphere is simply the actions and thoughts of humans, which is what makes it so unprecedented.

Unlike the law of a tribe, which was always defined by some legendary event in or an agent in "higher" time, the public sphere is seen as nothing more than the communication of men. Thoughts that arise today is thought of as no different in nature from what happened previously in the public sphere. This way, it becomes fully secular, without being based in the transcendent.
